Understand signing, witnessing and certified copies

Some legal documents must be signed, witnessed, sworn, certified or notarised before they can be used.

This page explains what those words usually mean.

Always check the instructions on your form. Some documents have special rules.

This service gives general information. It does not give legal advice.

Signing and witnessing a will

A will must be signed correctly.

In Barbados, a will must usually be:

  • in writing
  • signed by the person making the will, or by someone else in their presence and by their direction
  • signed or acknowledged in front of 2 or more witnesses who are present at the same time

Each witness must also sign in the presence of the person making the will.

Do not sign a will before the witnesses are with you.

Do not choose a witness who will receive anything in the will.

Do not choose a witness who is married to someone who will receive anything in the will.

If a witness or their spouse is given something in the will, that gift may fail.

You should speak to a lawyer if:

  • you are not sure how to sign the will
  • someone is pressuring you to sign
  • someone may challenge the will
  • the person making the will cannot sign
  • the person making the will cannot read or understand the document
  • you are not sure who can be a witness

Signing a document

Signing means you add your signature to show that the information is yours or that you agree with what the document says.

Some forms must be signed in front of another person.

Do not sign a form before meeting the person who must witness it, unless the form instructions say you can.

Witnessing a signature

A witness watches you sign a document.

The witness may also need to sign the document and add their name, address, job title or date.

Some forms say who can be a witness.

For a will, the witness rules are important. A person who receives something in the will, or that person’s spouse, should not be a witness.

Swearing or affirming a document

Some documents must be sworn or affirmed.

This means you confirm that the information is true.

A sworn document may need to be signed in front of a person who is allowed to take oaths.

An affirmation is similar to an oath, but it does not use religious wording.

Affidavits and statutory declarations

An affidavit is a written statement of facts that is sworn or affirmed.

A statutory declaration is a formal written statement that you declare to be true.

Always check the form instructions. Some documents must be sworn or declared before a specific person.

Certified copies

A certified copy is a copy of an original document that has been checked and marked as a true copy.

You may be asked for certified copies of documents such as:

  • birth certificates
  • marriage certificates
  • death certificates
  • ID documents
  • court documents

The person certifying the copy may need to see the original document.

Notarised documents

Some documents must be notarised by a notary public.

A notary may witness signatures, authenticate documents or issue notarial certificates.

The Supreme Court guidance explains who can perform notarial acts in Barbados and the relevant fees.

Apostille certificates

An Apostille certificate may be needed if a Barbados document will be used in another country.

Always check whether the country or organisation receiving the document requires an Apostille.

Storing the will

After a will is signed and witnessed, keep the original somewhere safe.

Tell someone you trust where to find it.

The Records Branch is part of the Barbados court system. It provides a free safekeeping service for original signed wills.

The official guidance says the original will must be placed in a sealed envelope.

The envelope should include:

  • the date of the will
  • the name and address of the person making the will
  • the names and addresses of the executors

This is for safekeeping. It does not mean this prototype checks that the will is valid.
